Family 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦

24 collocations about family relationships ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Family

Today's topic: family relationships. Key collocations include immediate family, extended family, close-knit family, dysfunctional family, broken home, carefree childhood, troubled childhood, bitter divorce, messy divorce, divorce settlement, joint custody, sole custody, child support, and stay on good terms.

"Tell me about your family! Who lives with you? Would you say you have a close-knit family?"

Reading: Anna's Story

Anna grew up in what most people would call a dysfunctional family. Her parents had a messy divorce when she was five — arguments, lawyers, a long and painful divorce settlement.

Unlike children with a happy carefree childhood, Anna had a genuinely troubled childhood. She grew up in what felt like a broken home.

Her father was first awarded sole custody, but later they agreed on joint custody. Her mother paid child support each month.

Her extended family became her safe space. Together they formed a surprisingly close-knit family unit.

Today Anna is a proud single mother of a seven-year-old boy, and her parents have finally learned to stay on good terms for his sake.

Relationships 💕

10 collocations about relationships and romance ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Relationships

Key collocations: make friends, close friends, fall in love, love at first sight, soul mate, significant other, have feelings for, unrequited love, childhood sweetheart, make a commitment, propose, have an affair, cheat on.

"Do you believe in love at first sight? Tell me about your closest friends!"

When you move to a new city, it can be hard to make friends. You'll probably have a number of casual acquaintances — people who you know on a basic level, but with whom you don't have a significant relationship.

Over time, some of these may become close friends. Do you believe in love at first sight? That's when two people meet and immediately fall in love.

Some people believe that their significant other is their soul mate. It's possible for one person to have feelings for another person, but if the other person doesn't feel the same way, then the feeling is not mutual.

Some people marry their childhood sweetheart. When the couple is ready to make a commitment, the man proposes to the woman.

Unfortunately, not all love stories have a happy ending. Sometimes a husband or wife has an affair. We can also say that they are cheating on their husband/wife.

Appearance 👀

Collocations for describing how people look ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Appearance

Key collocations: gorgeous, jet-black hair, pale blue eyes, radiant complexion, hourglass figure, slender waist, bear a striking resemblance, unkempt hair, ruddy complexion, deep-set eyes, bushy eyebrows, facial hair, athletic build, broad shoulders, trim a beard, somewhat attractive.

"Describe a friend or celebrity! What do they look like? Use some of these collocations."

My best friend Joanna is absolutely gorgeous. She has long, sleek, jet-black hair, pale blue eyes, and a radiant complexion.

People always compliment her on her hourglass figure and ask her what the secret is to maintaining such a slender waist. She has a round face with an upturned nose, and she actually bears a striking resemblance to a famous singer.

Her boyfriend has unkempt hair, a square face and a ruddy complexion. His deep-set eyes are almost hidden under his bushy eyebrows, and he sports a thick mustache and a huge beard.

His body isn't bad — he has an athletic build, with broad shoulders and muscular arms. If he would only trim his beard and comb his hair, I suppose he could be considered somewhat attractive.

Character & Behavior 🧠

15 collocations describing personality ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Character & Behavior

Key collocations: painfully shy, vivid imagination, outgoing personality, sense of humor, brutally honest, hurt feelings, fiercely loyal, show true colors, play a prank, bear a grudge, low self-esteem, mean streak, come out of shell, thicker skin, speak mind, superiority complex, swallow pride, put others first.

"Are you an outgoing person? Tell me about your personality — or someone you know!"

When I was a teenager, I was painfully shy. I loved reading books, and I had a vivid imagination. My best friend had a more outgoing personality and a good sense of humor. Unfortunately she was also brutally honest, and sometimes her comments hurt my feelings.

Although I was fiercely loyal to her, she showed her true colors when she played a prank that humiliated me. I bore a grudge against her for years afterwards. Now that I look back on it, I can see that she had low self-esteem and a bit of a mean streak.

I've come out of my shell since those days; I have a thicker skin and it doesn't bother me when people speak their minds.

My biggest flaw is that I have a tendency to be arrogant — some say I have a superiority complex. I'll admit that it is hard for me to swallow my pride and recognize when I've been mistaken. My resolution for the New Year is to put others first.

Feelings & Emotions 😊😢

10 collocations about emotions ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Feelings

Key collocations: roller coaster of emotions, ridiculously excited, worried sick, seething with anger, visibly disappointed, bottle up emotions, lose temper, deeply depressed, emotional wreck, pleasantly surprised, immensely grateful, overwhelmed with emotion.

"Tell me about a time you felt very emotional. What happened?"

I experienced a roller coaster of emotions when my son was born. During my wife's pregnancy, I was ridiculously excited about the prospect of becoming a dad.

On the big day, I'd imagined that everything would go smoothly — but there were complications during the delivery and my wife had to have emergency surgery while I waited, worried sick.

As time went by, I got increasingly anxious because the doctors weren't telling me anything. After two hours I was seething with anger, and I shouted at one of the nurses.

My wife was visibly disappointed that I wasn't by her side when she woke up from surgery. She prefers to bottle up her emotions — but I knew I had let her down and I was terribly sorry that I had lost my temper.

The months that followed were tough. My wife became deeply depressed, and I too was an emotional wreck as I tried to juggle the demands of work and family life.

But now things are starting to turn around — I've been pleasantly surprised at all the help we've gotten from our friends and neighbors. I'm just immensely grateful that my wife and son are healthy, and sometimes I'm overwhelmed with emotion as I reflect on how lucky we are.

I recently spent a month on vacation — but instead of staying in a hotel, I looked for a short-term rental. I ended up sharing a two-bedroom apartment with some friends. It was actually cheaper than renting a studio apartment all by myself.

But still, I'm gonna start saving up for a down payment. Someday, when I do move into a new place, I'll be sure to throw a housewarming party.

Lesson 10

Sports ⚽

15 collocations about sports ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Sports

Key collocations: play soccer, do yoga, go swimming, home game, away game, win the game, lose the game, tie the game, have the lead, make a comeback, enter a competition, personal best, break the world record, set a new world record, withdraw from the competition.

"What sports do you play? Do you prefer team sports or individual activities?"

We typically use play for team sports — you play soccer, play basketball, play tennis, play baseball, and play golf.

The verb do is used for more individual sports — you do yoga, do gymnastics, do martial arts, and do aerobics. Also, in more general terms, you do exercise.

The verb go is used with most activities that end in -ing: you go swimming, go biking, go surfing, go rock climbing, go bowling, and go fishing.

With team sports, we can use the word game or match: a soccer game, a basketball game, a tennis match, etc. You can win the game, lose the game, or tie the game.

A team can play a home game or an away game. When one team is winning in the middle of the game, we say that team has the lead. However, the other team can make a comeback and take the lead.

Athletes can enter a competition and try to win first place. If the athlete is performing well, they may achieve a personal best — and if they perform better than ANYONE ever has in the past, then they could even break the world record or set a new world record.

Lesson 11

Health & Sickness 🏥

12 collocations about health ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Health

Key collocations: life expectancy, quit smoking, reduce stress, get plenty of sleep, exercise regularly, health insurance, suffer/sustain injuries, break/fracture a bone, relieve the pain, chronic condition, over-the-counter medication, prescription medication, alleviate symptoms, catch a cold, terminally ill.

"What do you do to stay healthy? How often do you exercise?"

There are a number of lifestyle changes you can make to improve your overall health and increase your life expectancy — you can quit smoking, reduce stress, eat a balanced diet, get plenty of sleep, and exercise regularly. It's also important to have health insurance in case of an accident or serious illness.

If you're in a car accident, you might suffer serious injuries. Of course, if you're wearing your seatbelt, you're more likely to have only minor injuries. If you've broken a bone, the doctor will put your leg in a cast.

If you have a minor ailment like a headache, you can take some aspirin to relieve the pain. If you suffer from a chronic condition like allergies, asthma, back pain, or depression, there are both over-the-counter and prescription medications that can help alleviate the symptoms.

When someone has a minor respiratory infection, we say they have a cold. Don't get too close to that person, because you could catch a cold too!

If someone is diagnosed with a more serious illness such as cancer or AIDS, they'll need more extensive treatment. When a disease has progressed so far that it will be impossible to cure, then the patient is said to be terminally ill.

I'm teaching my grandmother how to use a computer. First I showed her how to start up and shut down the computer. Then, I helped her get online so that she can check her e-mail.

It's also a good idea for her to back up her files so that she doesn't lose her data if the computer crashes and can't be repaired.

Lesson 13

Study 🎓

12 collocations about education ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Study

Key collocations: apply to colleges, get good grades, be awarded a scholarship, take classes/courses, take notes, give/make presentations, hand in work, grade assignments, take tests/exams, pass/fail a test, withdraw from/drop a course, major in, graduate from, get a diploma.

"Tell me about your studies! What did you major in? What was your favorite subject?"

Most children begin their studies at 5 or 6 years old in pre-school, followed by kindergarten. After that comes primary/elementary school, which consists of first grade through fifth grade. Then there's middle school — sixth grade through eighth grade — and high school, which is ninth grade through twelfth grade.

After high school, you apply to colleges to see which ones will accept you. You send in/submit your application and then wait for the results. If you got good grades in high school, you might be awarded a scholarship to help pay the tuition.

In college, you'll take classes on various subjects. You should always take notes as the teacher is giving the lecture. Some teachers may ask the students to give presentations or do research and write an essay, an assignment, a report, or a paper. After you hand in your work, the teacher will grade the assignment and give you feedback.

To evaluate your progress, you'll take tests. If you get the minimum number of points required, you've passed the test; if you don't get the minimum number of points, you've failed the test.

If the course proves to be too difficult, or if you don't like it, you can withdraw from the course.

Most college students choose a major. To talk about a concentration of studies, you can use "major" as a verb or a noun — "I majored in biology."

When you graduate from college, you'll get a diploma. Afterwards, you can say you have a degree in the area of your concentration. You can have a bachelor's degree, master's degree, or doctorate (Ph.D.).

Lesson 14

Academic English 📝

10 collocations for academic writing ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Academic English

Key collocations: make an outline, gather evidence, support claims, propose a theory, refute a theory, touch on issues, draw a distinction, draw parallels, fall into categories, make your case, first draft, final draft, carry out a study, key factors, play a role/part, draw conclusions, raise questions.

"Have you ever written a research paper? What steps do you usually follow?"

Before you begin writing a paper, it's a good idea to make an outline of the main points you plan to present. The next step is to gather evidence to support your claims, since your work won't be credible if you propose a theory (or try to refute a theory) without some proof.

If your paper is going to touch on several issues then you'll need to be especially organized. You can draw a distinction between topics that are different, or draw parallels between examples that are similar. Another way to organize your ideas is to show how they fall into different categories.

After making your outline and gathering the supporting evidence you'll use to make your case, you can write a first draft in which you go into detail on the topic. Then, you revise the paper until finishing with the final draft.

Let's say you're carrying out a study on market trends. You can analyze the key factors that are influencing the economy, showing how local politics play a role in shaping the country's financial future.

At the end of your paper, you should briefly summarize the material you presented and draw conclusions based on your research. The end of the article is also a good place to raise questions for further study.

Work 💼

15 collocations about careers ⏱ 10 min
👩‍🏫 Teacher's Notes — Work

Key collocations: do an internship, entry-level job, minimum wage, team player, get a promotion, get a raise, be fired/dismissed, heavy workload, work overtime, demanding job, quit your job, competitive salary, generous benefits, career change, apply for a job, job ads, resume, cover letter, interview candidates, job description, dream job.

"What's your dream job? What do you think makes a good boss?"

If you've just graduated from college and you've never had a job, you can start your career by doing an internship to gain experience. Depending on the company, the internship could lead to an opportunity to get an entry-level job at the same company.

Although you might be earning minimum wage, if you do excellent work and are a good team player you could get a promotion or get a raise — hopefully both! Just don't slack off, otherwise you'll be fired.

It's normal for there to be periods of time when you have a heavy workload and need to work overtime. But if you have a very demanding job and you're stressed out all the time, then it might be time to quit your job and look for another one.

Now that you have experience, you'll be able to get a job with a more competitive salary and generous benefits. If you can't find a well-paid and rewarding job in your field, it might be time for a career change.

The typical way of applying for a job is to read the job ads in the newspaper or online, then send your resume to the company with a cover letter. The company will then interview the candidates it feels are most qualified, based on the job description.

If you're persistent, a great job offer should come your way sooner or later — maybe even for your dream job!
